Climate Change and Corn.

By Nicholas Le | 11/23/2022

"Average global crop yields for maize, or corn, may see a decrease of 24% by late century, with the declines becoming apparent by 2030, with high greenhouse gas emissions, according to a new NASA study" (Gray).

The Effects of Climate Change on Corn

Corn, or maize, is one of the most popular ingredients in foods all around the world from tortillas to cornbread to cornmeal. However, as global temperatures continue to rise and climate change worsens, corn may not be around for much longer.

In only about the last 40 years, from 1980 to 2020, the production of corn has decreased by 13% (Sharma). The reason for this change is that global warming and climate change is shortening the growth cycle of corn making yields less and less every single year.

Why is Climate Change Affecting Corn?

The most significant factor affecting corn production is rising temperatures (Islam). The Greenhouse Effect is the concept that all the air pollution and greenhouse gases, produced mainly by burning natural gases, is being trapped in our atmosphere and causing average global temperatures to rise. The is detrimental to corn production because corn needs to be grown at specific temperatures to be produced efficiently.

Another factor that's effects have been researched is precipitation cycles. Climate change brings about abnormal precipitation cycles and this has the potential to negatively affect corn production. However, studies show that these changes in rain have a negligible effect on corn yields and more focus should be put on rising temperatures (Sharma).

Conclusion/Call to Action

With the path that our society is currently on regarding climate change and sustainability, things will only get worse. Now, what can we do about it?

The most basic solution would be to reduce our greenhouse gas emissions. Now how do we do that?

Of course, developing new sustainable energy sources would greatly reduce our emissions and reduce the effects of the Greenhouse Effect. However, there are some small things that anyone can do to help fight climate change at an individual level. For example, taking public transit or carpooling or even biking instead of commuting individually to work/school. Another example would be to invest in solar panels or an electric vehicle.

In conclusion, all the problems being caused by climate change, such as the loss of corn, were caused by humans. However, this means it is also our responsibility to fix them. And the first step to solving this issue to for all of us to take individual action.
